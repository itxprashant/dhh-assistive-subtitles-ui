
Problem Statement
Hearing impairment is among the most common disabilities in India, yet deaf and hard-of-hearing (DHH) students in higher education remain critically underserved. At IIT Delhi, for DHH students depend on sign language interpreters: a support model that is expensive, availability-constrained, and introduces perceptual lag that disrupts learning. In classroom lectures, Microsoft (ppt) captioning is typically used. However, the technology performs poorly, with limited speech-to-text accuracy, making lectures at IITD largely inaccessible for students with hearing disability. No real-time, classroom-native captioning solution exists at the institute today. This is the reality across Higher Education Institutions in the country, despite the accessibility mandate under the RPwD Act 2016. This proposal aims to build a technology to change that.

Project Overview
In this project, we propose to build a standalone, plug-and-play device. A wireless microphone captures the instructor's speech. An on-device compute unit processes it through a fine-tuned speech-to-text model and a language post-processing layer. The resulting captions appear as a floating subtitle band at the bottom of whatever is already projected, without replacing or interrupting the existing content.

No internet. No cloud. No subscription cost. No change to how instructors teach.

Because it runs entirely on local hardware, it requires no data centre, no remote servers, and no energy-intensive cloud infrastructure. This makes it both a privacy-first and a low-carbon solution, in line with the growing global conversation around Green AI and responsible computing.

Novelty of the Speech Recognition System
Why existing tools fall short
    • Commercial STT services: require persistent internet, route audio through external servers, and fail on Indian-accented English and technical academic vocabulary.
    • Generic open-source models (e.g. Whisper large-v3): are trained predominantly on Western English and produce high word-error rates on Indian-accented speech and technical academic vocabulary.
    • No existing captioning product: composites output directly onto an HDMI signal as a native overlay layer. All current tools treat captioning as a separate screen output.

What We Will Build
    • Domain-adapted STT model: fine-tuned on academic lecture audio to improve accuracy on discipline-specific vocabulary and Indian-accented English, which base models handle poorly.
    • Two-stage pipeline: STT output is passed through a 2B-parameter language model for real-time grammar correction, punctuation, and formatting. Single-model approaches cannot match this output quality.
    • Sub-1.5s end-to-end latency: the full pipeline, from audio capture to caption display, is engineered to run within the perceptual synchrony threshold for DHH viewers.
    • HDMI-native overlay: captions are composited directly onto the projector signal. No second screen. No separate device for the DHH student. No action required from the instructor.

Hardware: Why a Refurbished Mac Mini M2 Pro
For prototyping our software, the Mac Mini M2 Pro is the only compact platform that can run a 1B-parameter STT model and a 2B-parameter LLM simultaneously within our latency budget, due to its unified memory architecture (16GB shared across CPU, GPU, and Neural Engine at 100GB/s). No driver setup. No SDK configuration. Development begins on Day 1.

We are deliberately choosing a certified refurbished unit. This is both a financial and an ethical decision: refurbished hardware costs 40% less than new, carries a warranty, and extends the useful life of a device that would otherwise have been discarded. Sustainability is a core principle of this device, from the local AI inference model that eliminates data centre energy costs, to the hardware choices we make at every stage.

LHC Pilot: What It Looks Like on the Ground
The Lecture Hall Complex (LHC) is where the need for this is most acutely felt. DHH students attending classes there currently contend with distorted room acoustics, large lecture hall sizes, and a complete absence of real-time captioning support. The optional Phase 1 extension targets 10 smaller LHC rooms first, where audio capture complexity is more manageable, before scaling to the larger halls.

Each of the 10 rooms sees a minimum of 80 students daily. The assistive unit sits inline between the classroom laptop and the projector, requiring no setup from the instructor and no special seating for the DHH student. The pilot will track caption accuracy, audio capture reliability, and student feedback across the full acoustic range of these rooms, generating the real-world data needed to confidently scale to the larger LHC halls in Phase 2.

Expected Outcomes
Accessibility Impact
    • DHH students gain access to real-time, classroom-native captions, reducing dependence on sign language interpreters.
    • The institution demonstrates measurable RPWD Act 2016 compliance, strengthening DEI standing with UGC and NAAC.
    • A live pilot across 10 LHC rooms provides immediate, visible impact for the 800+ students passing through those rooms daily.

Technology Contribution
    • A validated software solution demonstrating that on-device AI can deliver accessible captioning without cloud dependency, subscription cost, or infrastructure change.
    • A documented, replicable system architecture that any institution can adopt on existing hardware at marginal cost.

Project Team

The project is supervised by Prof. Vikram Singh, Department of Chemical Engineering, whose guidance has been instrumental in shaping both the technical direction and the accessibility focus of the work. Prof. Deepak Kumar, Dean, Diversity and Inclusion, has been deeply supportive of the initiative and continues to provide valuable direction as the project moves forward.

This project aims to address the institute’s accessibility requirements and support the creation of a more inclusive academic environment.
